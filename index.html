Common questions

Solar in the Philippines, answered.

Is solar worth it in the Philippines in 2026?

For most Filipino homes, yes. With grid rates high and hardware cheaper, a right-sized rooftop system commonly pays back in 3 to 5 years, then delivers years of largely free daytime power. The one number that settles it is the one on your own electricity bill — which is exactly what Apolaki works from.

How does net metering work in the Philippines?

You export surplus daytime energy to your distribution utility, such as MERALCO, in exchange for peso credits on your bill. Under Department of Energy rules, residential and small commercial systems up to 100 kW can qualify. Read our net metering guide →

How much does a home solar system cost?

A useful planning figure is roughly ₱45,000–₱55,000 per kWp installed. A typical 5 kWp Metro Manila home lands around ₱225,000–₱275,000. Apolaki shows an honest range with its assumptions, not a single hard quote. See the full cost breakdown →

What is the payback period for solar?

Payback is commonly 3 to 5 years, depending on how much of your solar you use as you generate it. The more daytime energy you self-consume, the faster the system pays for itself.

Will solar work on my roof?

Most sound roofs with reasonable sun exposure are suitable. Apolaki models your roof using NASA POWER and Google Solar data and flags shading or orientation issues, so you know before an installer visits. Check your roof suitability →

Can I finance solar in the Philippines?

Yes — many installers and banks offer solar loans. Apolaki reframes the upfront price as a monthly figure you can compare directly against your current electricity bill. Explore financing options →

Is my data private?

Yes. Your bill and address stay private. Only anonymised rooftop metrics reach an installer, and only when you choose to connect. Apolaki is built to comply with the Philippine Data Privacy Act (RA 10173). Read our privacy policy →
