Solar installers in Cebu — start from your bill

Cebu enjoys some of the strongest, most dependable sun in the country — a rooftop here returns close to 1,500 kWh a year for every kWp installed, meaningfully more than Metro Manila. At the same time VECO's residential rate climbed to roughly ₱12.88 per kWh by mid-2026, so the math on rooftop solar keeps improving for homeowners with their own roof — from the hillside villages of Banilad and Talamban to the coastal subdivisions of Mandaue, Talisay and Mactan. The one twist Cebu adds is salt air: near the water, panels and mounting need marine-grade, corrosion-resistant hardware to last. Local context

Electricity and solar in Cebu

Most of Metro Cebu is served by Visayan Electric (VECO) — its franchise covers Cebu City, Mandaue, Lapu-Lapu, Talisay and nearby towns, while outlying municipalities fall under electric cooperatives such as CEBECO. VECO's residential rate reached about ₱12.88 per kWh in May 2026, up from roughly ₱12.57 the month before, with the utility pointing to higher generation costs — and those rising bills are a big part of why solar has taken off across the island. Cebu also sits on the Visayas grid with strong, year-round irradiance, so a well-sized rooftop system can offset a large share of a typical bill. Under the national net-metering program you can connect a grid-tied system up to 100 kW and export surplus daytime energy — but VECO credits those exports at its generation rate, roughly half the retail rate you pay, so it is a partial credit rather than a one-for-one rollback; the biggest wins come from using your own solar by day. A 2026 DOE rule requires the utility to act on a complete net-metering application within about ten working days.

What to expect

On Cebu roofs

Cebu's building stock runs from the wide-lot, hillside homes of gated villages — Maria Luisa Estate Park and Santo Niño Village in Banilad, Maryville in Talamban, and the subdivisions climbing Guadalupe and Banawa — to condo towers around IT Park and Cebu Business Park and the coastal homes of Mactan, Mandaue and Talisay. The detached hillside houses are excellent solar candidates: generous concrete-deck and tiled roofs, room for a proper array, and strong midday sun, with Cebu delivering roughly 1,500 kWh a year per kWp installed — close to five peak-sun hours a day and a step ahead of Manila. Condo units share a building roof, so solar there hinges on the developer and property management rather than the individual owner. Two local realities shape the build: salt air near Mactan and the coastal belt calls for marine-grade, corrosion-resistant mounting and hardware, and while Cebu sits south of the main typhoon corridor it is not immune — Super Typhoon Odette (Rai) tore through the island in December 2021 — so wind rating and proper roof anchoring matter. As a planning guide, installed cost typically runs ₱50,000–65,000 per kWp (about ₱50,000 at the entry level), most homes land between a 3 kWp and 10 kWp system depending on the bill, and a grid-tied system with net metering commonly pays back in about three to five years. You will file a building and electrical permit with your city — Cebu City, Mandaue, Lapu-Lapu or Talisay — and apply for net metering through VECO, and your real figures depend on roof orientation, shading and daytime usage, which is exactly what the free assessment works out for your specific address.

Questions

Solar in Cebu — questions

How much can solar save on a Cebu VECO bill?

It depends on your bill and how much power you use during daylight, but with VECO near ₱13 per kWh many Cebu homes offset well over half their bill. Exported surplus is credited at VECO's generation rate — roughly half the retail rate — so the biggest savings come from self-using your solar by day. The free assessment estimates your savings from your actual VECO bill, not an average.

Which utility serves Cebu, and how does net metering work?

Most of Metro Cebu is served by Visayan Electric (VECO), covering Cebu City, Mandaue, Lapu-Lapu, Talisay and nearby towns; outlying municipalities fall under cooperatives such as CEBECO. Under the national net-metering program you can connect a grid-tied system up to 100 kW and export surplus daytime energy — but VECO credits those exports at its generation rate, about half the retail rate, so it is a partial credit rather than a one-for-one rollback. You apply through VECO, which a 2026 DOE rule requires to act within about ten working days, and file a building and electrical permit with your city.

Does Cebu's coast and salt air affect a solar install?

Yes. Near Mactan and the coastal belt of Mandaue and Talisay, salt air corrodes ordinary hardware, so panels and mounting should be marine-grade and corrosion-resistant. Cebu also sits south of the main typhoon corridor but is not immune — Super Typhoon Odette (Rai) hit the island in December 2021 — so a good installer specs a proper wind rating and roof anchoring.
