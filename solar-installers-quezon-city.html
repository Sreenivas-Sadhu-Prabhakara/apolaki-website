Local context

Electricity and solar in Quezon City

Quezon City homes are served by MERALCO, whose residential tariff sat around ₱14.5 per kWh in mid-2026 — one of the higher retail power rates in Southeast Asia and the main reason rooftop solar pencils out here. The city's inland location on the Metro Manila plain gives it roughly 4.5–5 peak-sun-hours a day, so a well-oriented array typically generates about 1,300–1,400 kWh a year for every kWp installed. Net metering is available for systems up to 100 kW: you export what you do not use during the day and MERALCO credits it — but at the lower generation rate (roughly half the retail rate), not peso-for-peso — so the biggest savings come from using your own solar power the moment you produce it.

What to expect

On Quezon City roofs

QC's building stock skews toward detached houses and mid-rise commercial rows, so genuinely usable roof area is far more common here than in the condo-heavy districts closer to the bay. Gated villages like Corinthian Hills, White Plains, La Vista and Ayala Heights sit on generous lots with concrete or long-span metal roofs that take panels well, while the denser grids of Cubao, Kamias and Project 6 still offer plenty of sun-facing pitches. Being inland, QC roofs largely escape the coastal salt-air corrosion that shortens hardware life in bayside cities — but mounting still has to be rated for southwest-monsoon (habagat) gusts and the typhoons that cross Luzon between June and November. As a planning guide, installed cost typically runs ₱50,000–65,000 per kWp (about ₱50,000 at the entry level), most homes land between a 3 kWp and 10 kWp system depending on the bill, and a grid-tied system with net metering commonly pays back in about three to five years. On permits, QC is one of the more organised LGUs: building and electrical permits can be pre-filed through the city's online ePermit portal, and the Certificate of Final Electrical Inspection from the City Engineering Office is what MERALCO needs before it will switch on net metering. Your real figures depend on your roof orientation, shading and daytime usage — which is exactly what the free assessment works out for your specific address.

Questions

Solar in Quezon City — questions

How much can solar save on a Quezon City electricity bill?

With MERALCO's residential rate near ₱14.5 per kWh, a right-sized system can offset well over half of a typical QC home's bill — most of the saving comes from powering the house directly during daylight, since exported energy is credited only at the lower generation rate. The free assessment estimates your savings from your actual MERALCO bill, not an average.

Which utility serves Quezon City, and how does net metering work?

All of Quezon City is served by MERALCO. Net metering is available for grid-tied systems up to 100 kW: you export surplus daytime energy and MERALCO credits it against your bill at the generation rate — roughly half the retail rate — so it offsets, but does not zero out, what you draw at night.

What permits do I need to install solar in Quezon City?

You need a building permit and an electrical permit from the QC City Engineering Office — both can be pre-filed through the city's online ePermit portal — plus the Certificate of Final Electrical Inspection, which MERALCO requires before it approves your net-metering connection. A reputable installer handles this paperwork for you.

Which Quezon City neighborhoods are best suited to rooftop solar?

Detached homes with unshaded roofs are ideal, so gated villages like Loyola Heights, White Plains, Ayala Heights and La Vista, and the subdivisions of Fairview and Novaliches, are strong candidates — but any QC house with a sound, sun-facing roof can qualify. The assessment sizes a system to your actual consumption rather than guessing.
